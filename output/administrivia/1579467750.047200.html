# Ivan Reese 2020-01-19 13:43
That makes sense if you think of it as "charity". But I think you might be fairly unique in seeing Patreon through that lens. Hard for me to say — the Patreons I support are done with the primary aim of supporting someone whose work I appreciate, so that they can continue producing the work. I don't think about it much more deeply than that. I certainly don't compare the value I receive from their work to the value I receive from, say, Netflix, since they're serving wildly different aspects of my quality of life.



Flipping the question around... Here are my "FoC Patreon" options, as I see them, based on the facts on the ground (like the amount Steve was making from his Patreon for FoC before he retired, and the current sorts of work I can do for the community).

1) I reward backers with something new, on top of the things I'm already doing — like a new kind of podcast, or a video series. That means the money earned from Patreon has to cover the cost and effort of all the things I'm already doing plus this new thing. Given that Steve's Patreon was bringing in about $250/month (before fees, expenses, etc), and assuming a new FoC Patreon would bring in a similar amount, it's hard to argue that this is worth doing — I'd just be pushing myself deeper into the red.

2) I take something I'm already doing and move it behind the paywall. That means people in the community are worse off than if I hadn't started a Patreon, except for those who are willing to pay (which will almost certainly be a small minority of people here). And those people who do pay are now paying for something they used to get for free, and that's usually a sour proposition.

3) I keep doing all the things I'm currently doing, for all the current audiences, and ask people to support me because they want me to keep doing these things. Nobody in the community is any worse off for not paying, I don't need to take on yet another ongoing responsibility, and I receive a token compensation for the hours spent doing the podcast, administration, promotion, etc. from the folks who see that as something worth supporting.

Given the small amount of money on the table, #1 seems unsustainable, #2 seems punitive, and only #3 seems reasonable. What do you think? What am I missing? What other options are there?

# Doug Moen 2020-01-19 20:09
FWIW, I donate to the Patreon accounts of a bunch of people based on the fact that I regularly consume their work, and I want to give back. I'm not motivated by Patreon "rewards", and the amounts I donate are not aligned with reward levels. This makes me wonder what percentage of FoC type people would not contribute if there were no rewards. You could run a survey on this slack. Will you consider donating to Ivan's Patreon to help fund this community? If there are rewards, will this affect your decision to contribute?

My own attitudes towards the Patreon reward system are probably conditioned by the norms of the open source movement. Maybe that's also true for a lot of other people in this community. I just dump my own work onto a public github repository without attempting to monetize it, just like all of the upstream projects I depend on. I don't have a Patreon fund, and if I did I would not want to spend much time managing it.
