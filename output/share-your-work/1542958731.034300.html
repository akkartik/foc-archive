# Steve Krouse 2018-11-23 03:38
@Duncan Cragg - your argument is compelling. Let me add some nuance so you can see where I'm coming from as well:

> Personally, for my own goal of programming for non-techies,

I have a similar goal, but while I used to be concerned with someone's first few experiences of programming, I am now not so worried if they need to spend a reasonable amount of time (similar the amount of time people spend to learn Excel) to get up to speed. I am now more concerned with the problem of normal people wanting to make small changes to large app that the use in a reasonable amount of time (which will take longer if they are not yet used to programming at all or in this new system).

> The real world

The is a common strategy, trying to argue that a language feature or paradigm is "closer to reality" somehow. For example, Alan Kay talks about how objects are great because they are like biological cells and cells are able to create great complexity, emerging from simple parts. That convinced me in the past, but I am now skeptical of those claims, because "like reality" is not an end in and of itself. The goal, I believe, is the comprehensibility of large systems and nature seems to resist comprehension, or at least be quite difficult to comprehend, a la quantum mechanics.

> has state,

There are a few different ways to handle state in FP. My preferred way is the original FRP, now called DCTP (denotative continuous time programming). I think it's truly beautiful, but it also took me months to fully grasp, so it's hard to communicate why.

Here's one way to understand it: it's like directly plopping the equations from your physics textbook into your text editor and then you have gravity. It's like math. No IO Monad required.

> visible everywhere,

Firstly, state in the "real world" is not visible everywhere... unless you're some sort of superhero with x-ray vision... if so, that's a different discussion... :wink:

Secondly, composibiliy is a really important property, and it's hindered by global state.

> and real "types" are in the eye of the beholder.

This is a fascinating claim. Types are a really difficult thing to pin down and define. In my head, they've always had to do with the shape of some data. I have an essay on it, here. https://medium.com/@stevekrouse/types-are-shapes-d6af1e83192f

If we use this metaphor to think through what you said, I think you would be incorrect because shapes are objective in reality. You simply cannot fit a round peg into a square hole.

I guess you could say that "round" and "square" are human-defined concepts... but if you want different concepts I guess you could make your own types and type classes

# Shalabh 2018-11-26 00:58
Now onto the meaty stuff:

In general I agree with @Duncan Cragg about state - it absolutely needs to be a first class citizen. It's not just about being easier to start with, but everything we use the computer for involves state - our 'final goals' are state oriented. E.g. 'send person X a message Y', 'find my picture from yesterday, remove red-eye', 'move $X from my account to my friend' and so on - things have name, state, mutation, history etc. I'm not saying mathematical functions are not useful - but that we have to start with state and then incorporate the functional transforms. E.g. if we have an object with name N, we could consider its transformation somewhat mathematically. The transform definition (~function) itself could be considered another stateful object with history and could live in the same world as the objects it mutates - rather than in an outside world.

I also agree with @Steve Krouse that IO is an implementation. Really this problem only appears if you first slice your system into programs because 'IO' is just messages leaving or entering your program. While if you look at the whole system, the only IO is what leaves the system, everything else is just messaging at the 'boundary of two subsystems' as Roly said? Orthogonal persistence also shares the idea that IO is an implementation.

Re comrehensibility of large systems, I feel what's most useful is inspectability - the ability to ask the system specific questions - ask what is connected to what; what behavior will change when you change something etc. Any model - OO/FP whatever will fail at scale because mental simulation wont scale up way. We already have some querying capability - source control tells us what files changed when, databases tell us what data changed, tracing/logging tells us sequences of some events, build systems can be queried for dependencies etc. But all of these are ad hoc and compose so poorly.

# David Piepgrass 2019-02-17 13:42
I like having a Lisp-style macro system available, but I think creating a macro system so people can write macros is the wrong approach. What I want from a macro system is a good set of well-designed macros designed by professionals, that everybody knows, so that you rarely need to write a new macro of your own.

Haskell, similarly, has a lot of good things to offer, but pure functional programming is not a form of thought that comes naturally, nor is the various abstractions based on higher mathematics.

I actually think it's important to teach some of these abstractions and probably someday they will be a part of every school curriculum - but I think a good programming language embraces diverse forms of thought - imperative, declarative, functional, domain-specific - and allows programmers to write programs in a way that resembles the way they think about the problem they're solving. The role of the programming language is not to enforce "purity", but to provide rich, intuitive functionality and to guide people into the "pit of success" - by making the probably-better way of doing things easier than the probably-worse way.

Short pit-of-success example: in languages like JavaScript creating a global variable is super easy, but a member variable must be accessed through "this", as in this.v. This should be flipped on its head so the member variable is easy to reach and the global variable is written global.v.
