# Paul Tarvydas 2026-01-07 20:26
Until Jobs commercialized some of what he saw at Parc, the only interface to commercial "home" computers was the MSDOS and CP/M command line. UNIX was essentially only available to students at universities, and it only had a command line interface. Even the Apple II was command-line based. You could create graphics, but as single apps, not GUIs that acted as the main HCI. I think that the Lisa was the first GUI-based home computer. Perplexity says:
> First commercial GUI: Xerox Star (1981, office workstation). ~$16,500.00 ($55,000.00 in today's money)
> First successful consumer home GUI: Macintosh (1984, $2,495).
> Lisa bridged the gap but flopped commercially (~100K units sold, ~$10,000.00).
Apple coupled the Mac with a Postscript printer and made the desktop publishing market accessible and affordable for "home" use.

Further, Perplexity says:
Timeline Context
• Macintosh (Jan 1984): Hardware GUI debut.
• LaserWriter/PostScript (Mar 1985): Enables DTP.
• Windows 1.0 (Nov 1985): Microsoft’s first GUI OS, tiled windows, mouse support; initial sales ~500K.
• X11, the first stable version of the X Window System protocol, appeared on September 15, 1987.
So, I would agree that the Parc GUI won out, at least in terms of the home-affordable computer. Apple targeted the "creative" markets, starting with better looking fonts and postscript, branching out to DAWs (buying out Emagic (Logic)). Microsoft targeted the "office" market. Apple carved out the "creative" market, IBM tried to push OS/2 and eventually gave up.
