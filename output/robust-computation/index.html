# Andrew Walpole 2019-07-11 11:23 #
@Andreas S. to echo Dave a little, but also from the perspective that I also had all of these questions not long ago. It's really tempting to jump into, "how do I do what I do today with computers on the MFM, robustly?" But I have had to get into the habit of checking myself a bit with these ideas. We have 80 years of evolved correct-and-efficient-only systems under our belts, resulting in our machines today. So hopefully we can lean on the idea that we know what's useful computationally, to not make it take another 80 years to implement these things in a robust, indefinite system, but also the long road will still be taken one step at a time. The other thing that has helped reframe these questions is that, what you see in many of Dave's demos, with atoms of elements doing really simple things, we have to really think that a handful of these elements won't often make a robust program. Instead, maybe thousands (or thousands of thousands more!) across many many tiles to really start to see programs that can act in a really robust and productive way. So the scaled viewport we're looking at right now is really zoomed in, and thus tough to even suppose answers to these bigger-picture questions.
# Dave Ackley 2019-07-11 09:28 #
Do I know how to implement all those things as we stand here today? Absolutely not, not in any confident way. There's a ton of research and design insights and multiple levels of stack engineering we need to do to earn our way there. But I have no doubt we can do it.. and the payoff will be indefinitely scalable.
# Dave Ackley 2019-07-11 09:25 #
Will such things be as efficient as some near equivalent directly implemented on CPU and RAM? Surely not. But -- if they are successful anyway -- they will be robust and movable and all that good stuff that we get by paying for a common indefinitely scalable infrastructure up front.
# Dave Ackley 2019-07-11 09:23 #
We can and will have all sorts of finite computations, bigger and smaller, inside an indefinitely scalable machine. Can we implement some kind of finite database? Sure. Some kind of finite programmable state machine. Sure.
# Dave Ackley 2019-07-11 09:21 #
Hi @Andreas S.. Thanks for the question, and just for taking a look. For an indefinitely scalable machine, I find my initial 'computational thinking' keeps getting turned kind of inside out by 'systems thinking'. Q: Wouldn't it be super inefficient to have to bring critical data to a computation from far away? A: Sure, so let's arrange to keep it close by. Q: But what if we didn't know we needed it ahead of time? A: Then how critical could it really be? Or: Q: How do even we get a 100MB image into the machine to process it? A: If the image is at all important, usually it'll already be in there; then see previous question. And so on.
