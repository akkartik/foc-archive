# Ivan Reese 2020-01-19 22:33
So far, "running" the community has mostly meant asking people for pain points and trying to figure out ways to salve them. That takes time, but not much, and I enjoy it. I'd eventually like to start new initiatives that might have a small monetary cost, but there's nothing happening on that front just yet, other than the possible move to a different discussion platform.

The podcast, however, has a meaningful time cost and monetary cost. It took about 40 hours for me to make the episode with Jack Rusher, and I'm on pace to spend about that same amount of time on the upcoming episode. Future episodes might take longer, as I explore ways to raise the production quality. Considering the number of people who listen to the podcast, it's worth spending that effort — 40 hours of my time for thousands of hours of listener time. Money wise, there's a monthly cost for the software we use to record the discussion, hosting costs for the episodes, and a cost for the transcription. Repl.it have been paying for the transcription, and I hope that continues.

# Ivan Reese 2020-01-20 13:16
> if you want to get financial support for FoC you will need to do it full time
I don't understand why this would be the case. Can you expand on this point a bit?

> if FoC is a platform to help push your own ideas
That's a goal for me only insofar as this community should be a platform to promote everyone's work. I want the "rising tide lifts all boats" outcome.

> why not continue to do it for free, and treat it as a labor of love
I'll happily do the Slack community management stuff indefinitely, patreon or not, since the effort cost is reasonable and I value this community a lot and want it to continue to grow and thrive.

I think the podcast medium has a good balance between what supporters are willing to pay for (patreons and sponsorships), and what you have to subject your audience to in order to earn that payment (brief plugs and sponsor reads) — far healthier balance than ads on the web, on TV, amazon referral links, tracking cookies, etc. So I think that the podcast will be perfectly sustainable on a small scale, even with my eventual goal of making it feel very professional. I love the podcast medium with a tremendous passion, and it's a great complement to all the other audio/video work I do as a job and hobby, and I most certainly don't want it to become something I try to milk for a 100x ROI. I just want to make good episodes, interviewing interesting people that our community will appreciate and enjoy listening to.

If you have an interest in podcasting yourself.. have you thought about starting up a podcast of your own? If you did something that was relevant, you'd have an audience here in this community that'd surely take an interest and give you early feedback.
