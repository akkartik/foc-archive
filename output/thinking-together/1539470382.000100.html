# Kartik Agaram 2018-10-13 16:05
> I’m trying to wrap my head around tasks where extrinsic incentives work well according to your definition — do you have an example for what you had in mind?

Pretty much anything in the "old economy", the things we did for thousands of years. It's trivial to count the number of shirts a worker sews in an assembly line, and even relatively easy to perform quality control on each shirt. But knowledge work increases the degrees of freedom of each worker manyfold.

> And if I understand you correctly, you mean that writing software does not fall into that category?

Yeah. The rise of open source software is a pretty strong hint here. There's something that changes when programmers can choose what they work on. Part of it is that their concerns become more long term. When it's just a job one is more inclined to take shortcuts, particularly if they won't be noticed for a few weeks or months (by which time you may have switched teams, or gotten promoted). But if it's your baby you think about it in the shower, you argue and agonize endlessly about tiny decisions. And the thousands of tiny decisions done right move the needle on externally visible quality.

Modern "20% time" and periodic in-company hackathons try to combine industrial organization with intrinsic motivation. You get paid, but you also get a looser leash to decide what to do. (But not too loose, because we don't yet know how to make it work.)

> Going back to @Gregg Tavares’s YouTube example: I think “YouTube would be 1000x better without money” triggered my response

At least the way I interpreted it, it was a statement of fact rather than values. When Youtube first started, the time of the original generation of Numa-numa memes (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KmtzQCSh6xk) it was uneven but often authentic and serendipitous. When they first started paying creators things got better for a time (so no, money is not all negative), but the competition for money drew in more people who wouldn't otherwise make videos. And it brought in the A/B testers. And all that over ten years has made it a thousand times worse than before the money. Money had benefits, and yes both benefits and drawbacks magnified, but in this arena the drawbacks have swamped benefits.

Or at least that's how I see it.

# Kartik Agaram 2018-10-14 12:18
Since you brought up a scifi metaphors, and since the metaphors we use have a powerful effect on our desires and goals, I'll throw out a couple of scifi metaphors that have influenced me for years. Not pushing back on anything you said, just a non sequitur in hopes of continuing the conversation.

a) While our hopes for the future tend to look like the Enterprise, all gleaming chrome and clean interfaces, I now hanker after something more like the Serenity (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kQtdfh2Uxoo), all the wires and ductwork hanging out. While it's not as nice to look at, it seems far more hackable and democratic. Habitable. (http://akkartik.name/post/habitability)

b) Asimov's Second Foundation, as a sort of less tangible counterweight to the tangible technological advancement of the First Foundation. The First Foundation was technologically advanced like the Star Trek universe, but people were still recognizably people in their ethical and cognitive limitations. The Second Foundation was Asimov's attempt to envision a world of better people. While he got the details understandably wrong (there's no reason to believe telepathy will make people better), I think there's something to the idea of changing ourselves to improve the efficacy of our tools. The change I imagine is to incentives. People already have huge incentives to not be anti-social, but only locally so. I'd like to live in a world where just as much attention goes to global consequences of behavior. This is a technology problem too, but the solution will look very different from our usual examples of technology. It may well end up being as simple as a checklist.
