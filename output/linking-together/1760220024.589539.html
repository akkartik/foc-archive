# Konrad Hinsen 2025-10-12 23:41
In my professional life, I have seen "coding is empowering", and I have changes in who gets empowered.

I did a PhD in computational physics from 1989 to 1992. At that time, computational physics was already a well-defined approach, but its community was very small and wasn't yet taken seriously by everyone else. My PhD supervisor started to see how coding was empowering, because he managed to solve problems using code that he had failed to solve by other means. He looked for students with come coding experience to make a step towards bigger computations. I was the first of these students.

Fast-forward 30 years. Computational science is no longer the target of new hardware and software developments. We use commodity hardware and software, made by others for other goals. We still write glue code, but it gets broken regularly by tech churn. Coding is no longer empowering scientists (with some notable exceptions). It's something everybody has to learn to some degree, and it's no longer a source of a feeling of empowerment.
# Christopher Shank 2025-10-13 12:18
> Is coding emancipatory?
@Kartik Agaram The framing of this question feels off to me because in our current circumstances the answer is inevitably no. For me, a more useful question is "how do we make computing liberatory?"

• computing being more broadly concerned with how we use computers than how we program them. For example, for the average person the computer is more of a communication device than a computing device. coding fails to acknowledge this.
• liberatory being more of an ideal, future-looking arrangement that inverts these long standing tropes of domination and oppression in computing. While emancipatory is part of that umbrella, it's more about dealing with pressing challenges and identifying strategies that subvert these dynamics.
I get the sense that our community's explorations in live/visual/malleable/end-user programming implicitly grapple with this question, but I haven't really seen any explicit political stances arise from it. Bhaskar Mitra's work on Emancipatory Information Retrieval (also their talk) and the Sapphire Punk Manifesto are quite inspirational to me, in this respect.
