# Edward de Jong 2019-05-14 01:58
I don't think coding bootcamps are a good place for new languages, because most of the camps i have seen are focused on getting people into commercial readiness of some kind and training on something that is bleeding edge would seem disappointing to the students because when they get out they will be likely compelled to use some very conventional language, as few beginners have design or language authority. Only powerful, established talent can buck the system and inject a new language, or the sole practitioner who doesn't care what anyone else is doing. After working on a gigantic C program for a year with 12 others, i was sick of the contamination and error injection caused by people changing interfaces, and sought a better language, and settled on Modula-2, which was so commercially productive for me i kept using it for 20 years. This is why smaller projects have very profitably used less popular languages. Even ancient technologies like LISP will sometimes show up inside products; in the hands of a skilled practitioner anything is possible.

that being said, it would be a lot more fun, and a lot easier, to train people in a bootcamp using one of the new languages which drastically reduces the total amount one has to learn. With a total of 20 function calls you should be able to build a chess program for an iPad and a web browser. And omniscient debugging makes it a lot less frustrating. This is the double whammy coming, which will be dramatic moment for the software industry; the combination of simplification with a major reduction in what i call the frustration of not being able to reverse an effect back to its cause.
