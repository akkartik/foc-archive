# Stefan Lesser 2019-12-25 14:03
@nicolas decoster Thanks for your kind feedback and the tweet! You bring up some excellent points:

In short, I think the whole last section about technology choices is mostly just stating my personal preferences which have more to do with my past experience than anything else. I consider them a lot less important (that’s why they go last) and I don’t think they have much impact on inventing a future of programming in general. They mostly have an impact on my own work, because if I chose technologies I don’t like or don’t know well enough, I’m just not setting myself up for success.

In other words, if your background and your heart is in web technologies, than you should absolutely pick that as the foundation for your projects.

The mobile over desktop thing I feel strongly about because I’d like to “skate to where the puck will be”, and I just don’t believe that everyday computing will happen much longer on a classic mouse + keyboard interface. It’ll still be around for decades mostly for us who have issues with leaving text editors and terminal emulations behind, and I don’t really have a problem with that. I don’t even think touchscreens are where it’s at and we’ll be soon entering the AR age, whatever that means. Most (non-programming) people have most of their computational needs being met on a smartphone today, and if I want to reach those people, I need to start where they are. I could be wrong, of course. But I’d like to take that bet.

On collaboration I have much more to say. But not now. And I will likely do that in a future blog post. I can tell you that there was a paragraph “collaboration” in all drafts and edits until I published it.

# Stefan Lesser 2019-12-25 15:13
@Duncan Cragg Ok, so my approach is kind of the opposite to “general first and see how it goes”, because I think starting with a goal of building something generic (as in Turing-complete) puts you directly into programming language land. Nothing wrong with that! But everybody is doing that already.

I like to look at large, creative applications for music production, video editing, or visual design as examples for creative tools that enable people to create quite a large variety of creative content. These tools might have grown surprisingly or accidentally or even intentionally turing-complete over time (see <https://www.gwern.net/Turing-complete> for context), but they started out with a focus of how to manipulate domain-specific data structures visually. That’s what I find interesting and promising. I’m not opposed to ending up with something generic and powerful and Turing-complete, but I’d like to get there organically and not mandate it from the start.

I also feel like setting it as a goal from the beginning frames the whole problem as “just” inventing another (visual?) programming language — and I find that limiting.

When you ask about DSL, did it even occur to you that I might not even look for a language? Most of us here would probably expect something language-like to come out of that process. But I don’t know really, what I’m looking for yet. I’m more looking into data structures and what the essence of structure is and how much more those domain-specific data structures mentioned above can be generalized.

I know that this sounds weird, and frankly probably not very promising, perhaps esoteric. Hence my attempts to explain where I’m coming from in these blog posts. I hope I can get everyone who’s interested enough to a place where they no longer think it’s weird, and then we can start discussing how promising it is.

# Ivan Reese 2019-12-31 12:16
I'm late to this post (coming via the newsletter) — sorry!

One kind of fun reading it is in looking at each of these principles and deciding where in the divide my own work falls.

> Tools for everyone > better tools for experts
In this case, my first reaction was to assert to myself, "I'm 100% working on tools for experts!" But upon reflection, I realized that I'm actually trying to do both.

I'm working on tools for everyone, that allow for the gradual development of deep expertise in those tools. Tools for new experts.

This is an interesting framing device, especially in light of the recent discussion about https://ralphammer.com/make-me-think/. When people say "programming tools for everyone", I think there's usually a tacit assumption that these tools will reduce incidental complexity as much as possible, sure, but then hide whatever complexity remains but the designer feels hinders approachability. I wonder if that's just my assumption, though, informed by existing examples of accessible but sharply curtailed "everyone can do it" programming tools — Shortcuts, for instance.

What do you (person reading this) think of when you read the phrase "tools for everyone"? Do you imagine people becoming dizzyingly virtuosic with those tools, or do you mostly imagine children and non-tech adults taking their first steps into computation and playing with data in new but relatively simple ways?
