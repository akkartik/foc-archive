# Stefan Lesser 2018-05-30 12:05
I was thinking recently that it would be great to have a list of values, sort of a manifesto, not unlike the Agile Manifesto, to at least have a discussion about the values we share, and to find out where we differ. I think a lot of research is opportunistic (and that's not a bad thing), but lacks a big picture of where we want to take this.

Ultimately, it should be something that allows developers of different platforms and languages to unite and at least discover shared values. One other thing we're incredibly good at is building all these silos of different language and platform communities, where we can all re-invent wheels all the time. I think there is a benefit in this language diversity, but it's hard to reap, because these different communities seldom talk to each other.

@Shalabh Speaking of reflection, the kind of liveness you describe seems to emerge as online/interactive visualization/representation tools applied to a system capable of reflective awareness. # William Taysom 2018-05-31 23:45
And, friends, is it liveness itself that we value or is it liveness in the service of, say, comprehension? I like liveness for its own sake, feeling connected, in the moment, jacked in. For comprehension, I recall a prodigious engineer capable of feely navigating swaths of Google – more like teleporting through it – armed with no more than a single large terminal window. In as much as comprehension is our goal, practices to improve our own thought patterns may be the best path.

# Shalabh 2018-06-02 14:38
@William Taysom I feel liveness/tangibility is important not just for comprehension but also things like immediate verification of ideas, easy exploration, accidental discovery etc. I agree that good thought patterns (mental models?) are useful as well, but wont a live visualization help greatly with forming good patterns (specially if the visualizations are well designed)?

In the end I think mental simulation is just not scalable in a way that tangible, live visualization is. The debate between mental/internal and visual/external forms kind of seems like the old debate against writing via Socrates - the argument being that learning writing would 'introduce forgetfulness'. I believe even people with very strong mental capacity will benefit if they can use a more powerful external medium to help them.
# William Taysom 2018-06-02 17:54
@Shalabh Accidental discovery is a good one. Though we're in the midst of the liveness choir here, you are sure to find many outside who don't share our enthusiasm – often from those who found success despite poor visualizations. (More than one of my university math professors felt diagrams were near useless. Should have asked, "what's that all that over the chalkboard then?") I suppose it's partly the curse of mastery: once you arrive you forget the scaffolding. Add to that an aesthetic preference for graceful proof arches with all the motivating scaffolding removed.
# Shalabh 2018-06-04 10:27
@William Taysom 'many outside' also believe things are going spiffingly well in the programming world :smile:. Why must scaffolding be all removed in computers though? It's a medium that can show different views to suit the changing preferences of the reader. Have succinct, elegant proofs that are explorable, visualizable, interactive etc. with various scaffolding readily available.

Thinking more about it, I don't find it as simple as 'after mastery you dont need liveness/scaffolding'. Just like after mastery, we don't give up writing because it's a powerful tool that helps us do deeper/better thinking. I think 'liveness' is a fundamentally different mode of working and more powerful than just writing. It's a spectrum like [mental_only] < [mental + static_writing] < [mental + static_writing + live_interactive].
