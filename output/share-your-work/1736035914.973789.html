FoC Episode 74 • Is the Whole Universe a Computer™?

Jack Copeland, Mark Sprevak, and Oron Shagrir ask this question in chapter 41 of their book, The Turing Guide. They split this question in two, first asking whether the universe itself is a computer, then whether the universe could even be computed. These are lofty, unanswerable questions, sure, but they encroach on our territory — philosophy, automata, nonsense. So, in our usual reverent style and with attentive pacing, the three of us explore the paper, the questions, the answers they choose to highlight, and even share a few perfectly reasonable answers of our own.

# Márton Gunyhó 2025-01-09 11:49
I just finished listening to the episode (loved the ending!), and I think I can pretty definitely say that the universe is not computable(TM). The most basic examples of quantum mechanics contradict this. The outcome of the double-slit experiment or the Stern-Gerlach experiment, or the time between decay events in a radioactive material is, as far as we currently know, completely, truly random. This doesn't exclude the possibility of the universe being a computer, just like how the electrons and silicon atoms making up a CPU are fundamentally quantum-mechanical but they are still acting as a Turing machine.

I was a little bit disappointed overall by how little quantum mechanics and quantum computers were discussed, though I understand if you feel out of your depth there :)
# William Taysom 2025-01-10 02:09
There are a few ways to interpret what’s going on with quantum mechanics. I don’t know of a consensus on which one’s right or how to tell. Others here will know better. Roughly I think it breaks out this way. Either quantum indeterminacy is…
• genuinely random or
• decided by hidden magic we don’t know understand or
• preserved distributing over the environment giving rise to many worlds.
I hear that the math is cleanest for many worlds. For random resolution, it’s hard to tell when exactly it happens. Hidden magic would be the most satisfying if the magic could be reduced to some mechanism.
# Márton Gunyhó 2025-01-10 09:17
I would say that's a reasonably good description. Wikipedia seems to list quite a few additional interpretations for QM, most of which I hadn't heard of. But yeah, basically there is no consensus, which is kind of funny as the 100th anniversary of QM is celebrated this year.

In practice most people do math according to the Copenhagen interpretation, which would be the "genuinely random" option. I don't know much about many worlds, but I think it's not been ruled out basically because it's so simple and it does explain everything. Many people (myself included) find it pretty unsatisfying.
