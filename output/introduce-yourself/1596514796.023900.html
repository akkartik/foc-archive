Please do! It's a question I get asked frequently when I talk about computational reproducibility (and that is rather often nowadays). I tend to reply with the principle-whose-name-I-always-forget that says that the expected future duration of an event is equal to its duration so far. (This is a maximum likelihood argument based on an uninformed prior, so it's not valid of you have more specific information.) So... if you want your code to be usable for twenty years or more (not uncommon in scientific computing), consider C, Fortran, Java, or Common Lisp. But I am not really happy with that answer.

# François-René Rideau 2020-08-06 18:56
But bear in mind that the question is not just did it survive, but how did it survive?

FORTRAN will survive 63 more years, becoming more and more of niche for physicists and legacy numerical recipes.

We can expect Lisp to survive 62 more years, but Common Lisp in particular to only survive 26 years during which its standard will never see an update. Rather it will continue to see tens of new dialects each with a small following, some to survive by being embedded in successful applications such as Emacs.

We can expect COBOL to survive 61 more years, and during those 61 years, to grow into more of a Lovecraftian horror grafted onto a primitive ancient beast.

C shall survive only 48 more years, its standard being updated every decade or so, becoming ever more of a niche, with ever more subtle undefined behaviors causing programmers to break everything in the name of ever more dubious optimizations with diminishing returns.

Java has only 25 years of life in it. First they said it was good for app-lets. Then they recoiled and said it was good for serv-lets. Eventually, they will realize it's only good for toy-lets, as its towering cesspool of engineered mediocrity is made obsolete by Artificial Mediocrity.

# David Piepgrass 2020-08-21 17:20
@François-René Rideau Just keep in mind that 'abstract nonsense' like category theory is, first and foremost, nonsense. It is utterly meaningless to a typical person. More generally, human language is lousy for describing anything that is abstract. Concrete examples are needed if only just to indicate what is being talked about. Motivating examples add a further emotional appeal, a reason why the topic is important. For example, if I propose the Patriot Act, there will be little interest until a motivating example like 9/11 comes along. The presentation must make the audience want it.
