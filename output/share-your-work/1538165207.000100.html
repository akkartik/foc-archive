# Brian Hempel 2018-09-28 13:06
When I, or others, say they want to reinvent “programming,” a next thought is: how much of programming? The diversity of computational tasks is rather large. I’ve attempted to build my own diagram of the centers where human motivations and technical concerns tend to simultaneously cluster. (The central “computer” is not a cluster, but a representation of the ubiquitous cross-cutting tooling). If anyone has any knowledge of pre-existing “taxonomies” of computer science, or any thoughts on the diagram, I’d be happy to hear it.

# Nick Smith 2018-09-28 18:45
I think this is a useful categorisation of applications. There's arguably some overlap between categories, e.g. Wikipedia can be used in an exploratory manner. I also noticed you've mixed activities and artifacts together, e.g. "data analysis" and "cars". I wonder if it's possible to tweak things to be more consistent.

Now that we've got this diagram, it would be interesting to come up with a second one that breaks systems into abstractions, e.g. "interfaces", "messages", "state", "behaviour". I'm not sure if there's an unbiased way to do this though, it probably results in making too many assumptions about how a system "should" be viewed.

# Nick Smith 2018-10-02 00:54
I actually feel like “consumptive” shouldn’t be there at all. It’s not very illuminating. Stuff like videos, podcasts, and new articles are static media through which there is little interaction and no reason why they couldn’t be delivered on paper, cassette tapes, or film reels. Wikipedia, on the other hand, is more about “information search and exploration”, which benefits from a computational substrate.
