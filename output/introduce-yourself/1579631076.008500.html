# Colby Gutierrez-Kraybill 2020-01-21 10:24
Hi, I'm Colby, I've been working on the Scratch backend systems since 2015, starting with the MIT Media Lab and we have just moved out of MIT and are officially in our own Scratch Foundation org. My coding history started in 1989 using Borland Turbo Pascal, and has been cross systems/paradigms since that point and in no particular order Perl to Ruby, Python, Java, C, C++, Go, Node, assembly, JavaScript, my own DSLs, and of course Scratch. My current focus is supporting the deluge of Scratch users needs. Long term, I'm hoping to help incorporate Scratch into a cross compiler/cross platform frameworks that allows the ability to use Scratch in much broader contexts. I am based in the Boston MA area. And I was brought here by someone else on the Scratch team!

# Colby Gutierrez-Kraybill 2020-02-12 04:15
Thank you for the suggestion @Eddy Parkinson . There is a feature of Scratch that is called a ‘Cloud Variable’. The discoverability is a tricky thing/hard currently. When you create a variable there is a selection to make it cloud based. This allows the creation of persistent high score tables.
# Colby Gutierrez-Kraybill 2020-02-12 04:18
There is a balance that we have to strike with it in terms of resources, the backend for it is limited to 128 digit numbers, and updated up to 10/s.
# Colby Gutierrez-Kraybill 2020-02-12 04:20
The limit to 128 digits is to limit ease of creation for things like chat rooms. And we aggressively drop/shape connections to hold to the 10/s because the current design of scratch makes it trivially easy to loop updates to a variable at thousands of times per sec
# Colby Gutierrez-Kraybill 2020-02-12 04:30
With those constraints we still handle between 500-1000Mbps of traffic in cloud variable updates alone
# Colby Gutierrez-Kraybill 2020-02-12 04:31
There are lots of areas to improve here for our specific implementation and how we design for it in the future
# Colby Gutierrez-Kraybill 2020-02-12 04:32
Expectations around how the variables are updated/queued for least surprising results and and so on. Discoverability also needs work
