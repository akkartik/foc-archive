Where I differ from the mainstream is that I am not a fan of the modern web. I'm a big fan of Jonathan Blow, Casey Muratori, the "handmade" movement, and the ideas of "mechanical sympathy". I favor returning to native apps over web technologies everywhere. I feel like I'm a weirdo for being interested in both "the future of code" and also interested in low level computer optimization, but I believe it's possible to have the best of both worlds (at least that's my dream)! I come down firmly on the belief that computers are like a musical instrument, and we don't need to dumb them down (and slow them down) with an ever growing pile of abstractions, we instead need to provide a structured ladder of abstractions that can enable more expression. # Kartik Agaram 2020-06-27 23:47
Welcome, I'm at least one other similar weirdo here. I care about the low levels not for performance optimization but to combat the complexity death-spiral all across the stack: http://akkartik.name/akkartik-convivial-20200607.pdf

Here's my take on "returning to native apps over web technologies": the only reason we moved to web apps was that we polluted native apps for consumers with bloated installers, dark UX patterns (toolbars) and DLL hell. Now that story repeats on the web as well, so there's really nothing special about web apps. They seem more sandboxed, sure, but we've gradually put more and more valuable stuff inside the sandbox to the point that the outside often no longer matters (e.g. Chromebook). App stores seemed for a time to solve the problem, except not really (e.g. https://twitter.com/jeremyburge/status/1275896482433040386). The core problem is giving people the tools to add constraints on program behavior -- in a way that encourages them to grow more sophisticated in such program management. Any approach that expects consumers to just consume is doomed in the long run. This is the problem we need to solve, and the web has nothing to do with it.

The web is unsurpassed for documents and hyperlinks. Using it for other stuff is just fuzzy thinking.
