# Katerina solstad 2018-11-26 07:38
Hello everyone. I am middle age woman who had many directions in life from studying math to becoming a visual artist, stage director and actress. Recently I am self educating myself on programming and facing the problem that I feel a lot of people will be facing since getting into “knowing” what to do with a computer and in the computer is unavoidable. There is a need of thinking in more abstract terms that would allow learning through common logic of human mind rather than education done in unfamiliar to non-computer oriented mind language that describes another unknown language that talks to unknown and sometime “scary” beast - computer. Languages I have learn so far are Javascript, Elixir, Python, (html,css ), some Golang. While I am introducing myself to languages I am growing in understanding on difficulties and trying to bring into my mind solutions how things could be explained in more “human terms” and logic. I hope participating in this group will introduce new paths for people to cross the gap from not knowing into knowing what to do and how to approach computer programming.
# Edward de Jong 2019-02-04 21:44
Dear Ms. Solstad, if your abstract algebra is good, programming will be easy. Frankly programming uses very little math; the necessary pre-requisites are not taught well in school however; you can go through calculus and not be exposed to what they call "discrete math", which includes set theory, boolean algebra, combinatorics, etc. You are entering the field at an odd time in history, because programming was once easier, but more limited. Now, with billions of computers around in all sizes, programming has become very challenging, and the haphazard way the dominant programming stacks were created has resulted in a huge mess. Javascript, which by many measures is the #1 language is a "hot mess" as they call it. We are at what i perceive was the tail end of OOP era, and are at the boundary of a whole new, much easier era, So it is a great time to jump in if you look forward a bit. One of the best ways to learn is to build something very simple. Find the absolutely simplest thing you can imagine, and do that. My first program was finding the area of a triangle. Motivation precedes achievement, and since you want to make more human things, perhaps you can apply your knowledge of stage directing and acting to think of some tool that would benefit actors to memorize lines, or tools synchronize lighting cues, or something that combines your real world knowledge, and programming. It has been somewhat tragic that computer programmers are often in charge of figuring out what to build, when they don't have knowledge of the outside world. You can be a very successful programmer, and never leave your bathrobe and slippers.
