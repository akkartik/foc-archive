# Hemi Edwards 2025-09-17 07:10
Hello everyone,

My name is Hemi Edwards. I'm a computing and technology enthusiast from Aotearoa/New Zealand. I am indigenous and belong to the Rongowhakaata tribe of the Maori. We occupy our ancestral lands on the East Coast of Aotearoa/New Zealand, though we have lost much in the process of colonization. We have too gained much from our engagement with the rest of the world. For most of us our primary language is English but there have been efforts to revitalize our indigenous language of Te Reo Maori, for the last 50 years. Many other indigenous peoples see our success as an inspiration for the rejuvenation of their own traditional languages and I believe that digital technologies offer much to support this mission. Speaking English grants us opportunities to meaningfully engage with the wider work, especially in forums like this where we can learn about new possibilities and ways to think and interact with the physical world.

I have been familiar with Bret Victor's work for some years now and have been excited about the potential for these new approaches to make computer interactions more meaningful, embodied, and accessible than currently. This is really important especially when it comes to economically deprived and socially marginalized communities like my people, who lost much from colonization and whose experience in the traditional school system was specifically intended to stunt our development as individuals and as a people. This has had ongoing generational impacts for my people. Language revitalization is just one aspect of current efforts to close the gaps in terms of learning achievement, equitable economic participation, and development of personal and social capacity.

My own culture is somewhat of a hybrid. We use both analog (continuous, flowing) spiral motifs called called the Koru common in tattoos and carvings and digital (discrete, binary), tukutuku and pakati patterns commonly used in weaving and carving. They intertwine the artistic and metaphysic dimensions of our culture.

"Starting around 1987, cybernetic models again shifted; this time to a synthesis of the competing opposites. The term "stable postmodernism" is appropriate in that the move resolves both technical and social tensions of the previous sharp reversal in these information dimensions.

When we last left mathematics, it had moved from the Euclidian linearity of the 1960s to the fractals and dynamical chaos of the 1970s. In the late 1980s these two approaches were brought together. This was nicely illustrated by the "multifractal" approach initiated by Uriel Frisch (Nice Observatory) and Giorgio Parisi (University of Rome) in 1985. Multifractals are shapes which cannot be characterized by a single fractal dimension. Since they have multiple dimension measures attributed to them, some of these can be whole numbers. Thus multifractals can incorporate both fractal and Euclidian structures. A similar fractal-Euclidian synthesis has developed in the use of Michael Barnsley's Iterated Function Systems (IFS). Because combinations of both fractal and Euclidian shapes can be created (based on Barnsley's "collage theorem"), by the late 1980s a corporation had been formed for commercial application to image compression (transmitting algorithms for producing the picture instead of the picture itself)."

@Christopher Shank. That sent my mind down all kinds of tangents. My people have a similar conception of time to that held by the First Nations peoples of North American. We have a whakatauki or proverb, which says, Kia whakatōmuri te haere whakamua, which translates as, "I walk backwards into the future with my eyes focused on my past'

"This whakataukī or ‘proverb’ speaks to Māori perspectives of time, where the past, the present and the future are viewed as intertwined, and life as a continuous cosmic process. Within this continuous cosmic movement, time has no restrictions – it is both past and present. The past is central to and shapes both present and future identity. From this perspective, the individual carries their past into the future. The strength of carrying one’s past into the future is that ancestors are ever present, existing both within the spiritual realm and in the physical, alongside the living as well as within the living"
https://researchcommons.waikato.ac.nz/entities/publication/d…

I was excited to read a book by the Australian philosopher Huw Price, which speculated that time's arrow that leads from the past to the present, and future may not be absolute, but actually allow for the future to impact the present, especially through quantum mechanics. So he's basically vindicating the intuitions of my ancestors.
https://global.oup.com/academic/product/times-arrow-and-arch…

New developments in theoretical physics appear to confirm Huw Price's conjectures.

"“The surprising part of this project was that even after making the standard simplifying assumption to our equations describing open quantum systems, the equations still behaved the same way whether the system was moving forwards or backwards in time,” says first author Thomas Guff."
https://tinyurl.com/unhhn4yy

There is increasing evidence to support this theory too.

# Hemi Edwards 2025-09-21 00:31
While I am not a computer engineer, I was fortunate enough to secure a job at the Strong AI Lab during the pandemic, after engaging in a conversation about the impending impacts of AI technology and automation on my people with my former boss Professor Michael Witbrock who created a job specifically for me. My role was primarily on the policy aspect of AI and engaging with the community, especially indigenous Maori. I no longer work there, but I have maintained a connection to the lab and former colleagues. We've talked about various possible opportunities for collaboration, though nothing has actually eventuated yet.

https://www.ai.ac.nz/

More recently I came across a Linkedin post of someone who was concerned with the impact that AI tools like ChatGPT will have on the learning ability and educational performance of students that use it. I had an intuition that indigenous peoples are all too familiar with the effects of the introduction of new technologies for communication and sharing of knowledge. The prioritization of literacy, all but destroyed our oral cultures and disrupted cultural practices that had enabled the accumulation of knowledge and wisdom over thousands of years.

This was the explicit aim of the colonial government of this country which they enacted through the Tohunga Suppression Acts. The law allowed the government to prosecute practitioners of traditional healing arts and divination. The legislation was explicitly discriminatory, because there was no such legislation that targeted European practitioners of medicine or astrology, which was very popular at the time. The purpose was very clear

https://tinyurl.com/yc6u4cc8

I was curious whether anyone else had similar thoughts and I was really excited to come across Lynne Kelly's and Tyson Yunkaporta's work on reviving embodies systems of knowledge memorization and recall, that my ancestors also practiced prior to colonization and the suppression of our tohunga or wise men.
https://tinyurl.com/mpmkykz9

I speculated whether artificial intelligence could be used to aid in the acquisition of these skills or enhance our capabilities beyond what even our ancestors capable of. I randomly discovered that I was presaged in my speculations by a techno-utopian psychologist and computing researcher Warren Brodey, who was a contemporary of Minsky and Paper. He built the Environmental Ecology Lab just down the road from MIT in Boston. The distinction between augmentation and enrichment, especially resonates with me and I think would really appeal to Bret Victor too, if my interpretation of his work is accurate.

"The distinction between the two paradigms is subtle, but crucial. Augmentation is like using GPS to navigate unknown terrain: it helps us get more done, faster. Yet the technology drives the productivity boost, not us. Such gains are fleeting; without technological crutches such as our GPS-enabled mobile phones, we are as helpless as before. Enhancement, on the other hand, leverages technology to develop new skills. Imagine honing an innate sense of direction through advanced memory techniques or learning to read natural signs and celestial patterns. In essence, augmentation deskills us in the name of efficiency, while enhancement upskills us, fostering a richer interaction with the world. This fundamental difference shapes our integration of technology, determining whether we become passive operators or creative artisans."
https://mondediplo.com/2024/08/07ai-cold-war

I am personally of a mind, that artificial intelligence is a powerful enabling technology for indigenous peoples. We are inundated by a dominant, alien culture and forced to negotiate the world on their terms. Engaging with abstract concepts like property, the law, markets, and institutions, that are completely foreign to us and yet they materially impact us. I believe that AI could enable our people to engage with this world on a more equal footing and that we bring a lot of value that could enrich the development of a technology like artificial intelligence.

I'm particularly intrigued about how languages and culture fundamentally affect how we think.

"Follow me to Pormpuraaw, a small Aboriginal community on the western edge of Cape York, in northern Australia. I came here because of the way the locals, the Kuuk Thaayorre, talk about space. Instead of words like "right," "left," "forward," and "back," which, as commonly used in English, define space relative to an observer, the Kuuk."
https://tinyurl.com/yr7snbpj
