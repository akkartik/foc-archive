# Srini K 2020-09-01 10:23
oh boy, I feel like there are SO many factors here. Here’s all things I think about :

• First filter, should I even process this? What medium / context was it given through? Social media feedback I’ve found to be anecdotally very useless. A long Github comment with helpful context or suggestions is better. Is the feedback personal / a personal attack? I almost immediately walk away!
• Assuming it made it past the quality filter.. I think about their context and my context. If someone is complaining about an issue but doesn’t meet my targeted personas I’m supporting (even though we dream of supporting the amorphous ‘everybody’, there’s always some optimized set of personas IMO) then I try to factor that in. It could be that their feedback is reasonable, but not relevant to my own goals for the project.
• What’s the situational context. Are they a customer / potential customer or an OSS contributor, a user, or just a critic? For the first one I agree with being very nice and thankful and friendly and maybe seeking more clarity.
• What’s the underlying / hidden truth or kernel in what they’re saying? This is something product managers know well. People say things like “I want to do X feature on your site locally” but sometimes they really just want the speed of the feature to be FAST on your website. So you may have to dig deeper.

# Chris Rabl 2020-09-01 17:03
I think @Srini K brings up a great point: there's a distinction to be made here between negative feedback, and critical feedback. Purely negative feedback ("this is crap", "why would you waste your time on that?") usually comes from people who wish you ill, but the best critical feedback comes from those who want you to succeed, and who are well-intended in their criticisms (that's not to say that this sort of feedback can't be incisive -- it often is, but i'll deal with that later). In my view, the only feedback that you should consider acting on is that which comes from the sorts of people who mean well. But just like hearing your mother shouting "clean your room!", it is often difficult and uncomfortable to deal with such advice, and it often comes across as curt or abrasive. The best way I've found is to look at feedback, not as a criticism of my "self", but rather as being critical of some work I've produced or some action I've taken. That may sound cliché, but if someone's well-intentioned criticism of your work bothers you deeply, you need to examine your personal reasons for why that is the case. Like Marcus Aurelius said: “we all love ourselves more than other people, but care more about their opinion than our own” :deciduous_tree:

# Chris Rabl 2020-09-01 21:20
Advice from people who are sincere toward you is not more or less liable to be objectively true, it’s just likely to be more carefully considered and deliberate than feedback that comes from someone whose intentions are less-than-sincere.

When it comes to responding to feedback with grace, that’s definitely an art, and one that I need to practice on a daily basis. In addition to putting myself in the feedback-giver’s shoes, it’s helpful to do things to moderate my reactions in the moment: “pressing the pause button”, so to speak, is super underrated. Whenever possible, I try to wait a length of time that’s proportional to the intensity of my initial reaction to a criticism I get before I respond to it. In cases where I’m very firmly invested in the work that I’ve done, this might be days, but in cases like this (where I’ve received good feedback, and the downside to sharing my reaction is fairly limited), a couple minutes is often enough. I find this most challenging to do in face-to-face settings, which is the reason I tend to solicit feedback primarily in written form (which has mercifully become more acceptable as a result of certain world events). If I don’t think I can respond to a given piece of feedback in a manner that I would appreciate coming from someone else, that’s my cue to dig deeper into why I had that reaction.
# Jack Rusher 2020-09-02 00:50
@Chris Rabl's advice about spending time with your feelings and trying to understand them before reacting reflexively is great in... (checks notes)... every other aspect of your life. It's the sort of thing that primary education ought to teach, but doesn't.

In terms of "the expectation is that criticism would bother you", it is normative to feel prickled by criticism, but the more productive framing is gratitude for an opportunity to improve this thing about which you care so much. There's nothing easy about moving from defensive to open hearted, but it's one of the most worthwhile things you can do.
