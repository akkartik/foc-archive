# Christopher Shank 2025-11-18 12:27
There's this great paper on how the PC revolution became co-opted and it describes a couple of different strategies for how it can happen.

> In a regularization strategy people try to fix social reality in the terms laid down by the dominant ideology, controlling social situations so that the ideal principles of social life become real and palpable.
> Adjustment strategies seek to redress this stigma, not by rejecting the dominant value system, but by attempting to portray the marginal personas a valuable human being within the dominant value framework.
> Reconstitution strategies stem from a conscious attempt to rewrite the rules, change the dominant ideology, and reshape the social context. Such strategies frequently make use of symbolic inversions or reversal.
I've been thinking about how such a framework applies to FOSS:
• the free software movement used a reconstitution strategy aiming to challenge the dominant ideology of closed/proprietary software with inversions that source code should be freely available, modifiable, and unable to become proprietary (copyleft). It's worth noting though that free software was a reactionary movement, aiming to recreate the golden era of software of the 60s/70s, where all software was in the public domain. I think such nostalgia failed to account for the starkly different environment that was, particularly with the advent of microprocessors and digital copyright.
• The open source movement looked at the reconstitution strategy of free software as a failure because businesses (run by business people) misunderstood what "free software" meant. I totally agree with you that the early OS advocates wanted to carry forward the ideals of the free software movement. But they did so with an adjustment strategy, figuring out how to market free software within the dominant system of business and proprietary software. Adjustment strategies carry an ambivalence towards the dominant ideology, and I think for open source that (eventually) meant departing the "ideological" copyleft licensing of free software towards more permissible licensing that proprietary software can use. Thus marking the beginning of how open source became enclosed and extracted.

# Jack Rusher 2025-11-20 10:54
In the mid 80s we wanted the software we used to be better and more “user serviceable”than the junk we got from vendors like Data General, and much of it was open source avant la lettre because it benefited us. Stallman’s crusade was off to the side of things like BSD, &c., and many of us believed at the time that less encumbered software was simply more free (“here it is, do what you want with it”). We did not at the time foresee a future when large corporations would run on our code without giving support to the people writing and maintaining it. In some sense we succeeded beyond our expectations and are now being punished for it.
