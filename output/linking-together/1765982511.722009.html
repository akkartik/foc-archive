I discovered the manifesto via this thread. First impression: well-intentioned but naive. The critique cited by @Ivan Reese points out the missing politics, and Conway's law explains why you can't ignore the politics and focus on just the tech.

# Konrad Hinsen 2025-12-19 01:01
Thinking of my old favorite again: Conway's law. What organizational and governance structure would actually support the development of "resonant computing"?

With the latest news from Mozilla, I see another wave of hope for Servo liberating the Web from corporate greed. But what does the Servo Web site say in its headline?
> Servo aims to empower developers with a lightweight, high-performance alternative for embedding web technologies in applications.
Empowering developers writing apps isn't really aligned with the goals of humane tech, resonant computing, and whatever other labels exist in this space. Its history with Mozilla Corporation, and its current attachment to the Linux foundation, also says "corporate tech". However, I know nothing about how the Servo team/community (which one fits better?) actually works.

I even wonder if Web governance, with central control by the W3C, is actually robust enough against corporate takeover to support humane tech.

So far for organizational structures that don't work. Which ones do? Personally, I'd bet on small protocols and small tools that implement and bridge multiple protocols. No organization big enough for corporate takeover to be able to harm the whole ecosystem. But do we have actual examples of approaches that seem to work?

# Ivan Reese 2025-12-29 07:01
> Software can now respond fluidly to the context and particularity of each human—at scale.
A lot hinges on this sentence. I think the sentence is dubious, even without the "at scale". I don't think the lived experiences in question fulfill the first half in ways that non-AI tools fail to.

I think AI is making easy things easier and hard things harder. "I vibe coded my own workout app" is something a person would say, but I think all the people actually saying that know enough about coding that they could also have just coded their own workout app. And nobody is using AI to free themselves from Apple's walled garden or fix Notion's buggy editor.

# Tomas Petricek 2026-01-04 13:30
There is a debate between Christopher Alexander and Peter Eisenman (who did a PhD on formal grammar for architecture in the 60s, then designed a couple of formalistic houses and recently designed the Holocaust memorial in Berlin) which seems to be pretty directly about resonance / dissonance (although using the word harmony):

> PE: If you are only searching for harmony, the disharmonies and incongruencies which define harmony and make it understandable will never be seen. A world of total harmony is no harmony at all.
and one more quote:
> I am suggesting that disharmony might be part of the cosmology that we exist in. (...) I am trying to find a way to deal with that anxiety. An architecture that puts its head in the sand (...) does not seem to be a way of dealing with the present anxiety.
It may be worth a read, because I think it hints at another critique of the resonant manifesto: http://www.katarxis3.com/Alexander_Eisenman_Debate.htm
