# Dan Stocker 2019-07-26 12:20
Hi all, here’s my take on visual coding: flowcode.kwaia.com. A work in progress, with an early September demo release in sight. At this point you can explore the structure of an example program. (The demo of an SPA rendering engine.)
The goal is to create a general purpose visual code editor geared towards touch devices, with a focus on usability and aesthetics. Also, with the use of a couple of standard libraries, to help developers build fast, robust, and secure apps.

# Ivan Reese 2019-07-26 20:54
That's a good start, Dan.

One thing I'd caution against is using the force-directed graph approach, where the edges have a desired length and you constantly iterate toward that. The problem with it is it makes the nodes very hard to confidently work with — things never feel organized, and you don't get any of the benefit of peoples' spatial reasoning because there's no way to put something in a spot and know it'll be there for you when you come back.
# Ivan Reese 2019-07-26 20:55
I'd also suggest throwing user-select:none (might need some vendor prefixes) in your CSS, so that as people are clicking and double-clicking and dragging around they don't keep selecting the text in your GUI.

# Dan Stocker 2019-07-26 23:27
Thanks for the feedback so far. Good questions / concerns!

@Nick Smith: I’ve been programming dataflow graphs for some time in TypeScript, and what I found even more difficult than comprehending a graph is imagining it. :smile: Yes, the “forced” focus is part of my plan to avoid graph hell, like the Division’s AI system that was posted to #linking-together recently.
I agree, arrows are not the best, and I have a few alternatives in mind. Need to experiment more.

@Ivan Reese: I agree that the layout should be more deterministic, ie to find things the way you left them. From my experience though visualizing programs in yEd for instance, when the graph is constantly changing, re-arranging stuff ends up taking an awful lot of your time. Auto-layout can be completely disorienting sometimes. With force direction, (or something similar) I intend to consciously take away the developer’s freedom to put things wherever they want, but at the same time not disorient them. Take it as trading spatial reasoning for spatial recognition.
This part may be controversial, but since I’ll be eating my own dog food building demo v2 in v1, I’ll be the first to know if the hypothesis doesn’t stand. :wink:
Re. touch, I’m testing the app primarily on an iPad.

@andrew blinn: Thanks, I’m happy it made a good first impression!
