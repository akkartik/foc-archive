# Stefan Lesser 2019-12-21 04:46
Over the last year I've spent a lot of time outside my area of expertise, mining some seemingly unrelated fields for inspiring ideas that can be combined with what we try to do here. One particularly deep rabbit hole I fell into was linguistics. As I'm slowly climbing my way back up into our world, I started trying to put some of the insights I bring with me from down there into a blog series.

My goal is to make some of the concepts I learned about more accessible to us designing and developing software. I think there is a lot that can be useful for dreaming up and building a more humane and democratized future of programming.

I start with very basic stuff, and go slowly, so there isn't much yet. I also try to keep the posts short (500-1000 words, ~3-6 minutes reading time). There are three of them right now, and you can start here: https://stefan-lesser.com/2019/12/06/structure-and-behavior/

Would love to hear what you think. Thank you!

# Edward de Jong 2019-12-22 01:19
I think you are approaching it with the correct philosophy. Certainly there is no need for most of the ceremony in programming today. If you think of programming as a linguistic system that is similar to human languages, there are only a few hundred verbs total in the english language, and people only use about 100 of them, and then there are vast numbers of nouns, and then various other parts of speech, and punctuation. Programming languages, the verbs are typically in the syntax of the language, and the nouns are the names we give to all the different variables and functions. One can see in modern systems that the biggest burden of learning is the supporting library API functions. In OSX there are probably 10,000 API's you can call. A vast space that is very difficult to learn. MS .NET has thousands as well. And since the API's have to be used in proper sequence, there is a massive amount of learning in every framework. You can read a newspaper in Chinese once you learn about 2000 characters, even though the total set is 50,000. Same with API's; you can build quite a few products with a small set, but knowing which ones to study first is not obvious to a beginner.

# nicolas decoster 2019-12-23 04:08
And if you read French, I recommand this book by Gilles Dowek (a french computer scientist and philosopher) : "Ce dont on ne peut parler, il faut l’écrire". Let me try to translate the title: "what you can't tell by speaking you have to tell by writing" (hope it is close enough to te Dowek idea!).

It is a very interesting book wich compares natural languages to other languages. In fact in French we have two separate words: "langues" are natural languages (English, French...) and "languages" are all other languages (math notations, music scores, programming languages...).

Natural languages were first spoken before written, and others were create to write stuff that benefit in expressivness when written. In fact writing was "invented" not to write down what humans were talking about, but to count things, it was a very utilitarian use. It helped us a lot in the action of counting and doing business.

I found very inspiring the separation of the two kinds of languages, and what is different and similar.
