# Xandor Schiefer 2020-06-16 08:08
> I hope you don't mind me asking, what is the motivation for using a functional subset of JS? Is that to provide "safer"/simpler experience to developers? And/or is it to enable you to build the next step, the visual editor, on a good foundation that matches the visual way of representing the web app?
It is for both of these reasons. Purely functional code is very declarative, and thus is much better suited to a visual representation. I also want a safer/simpler experience for developers. The final vision I have is very ambitious, and may not be feasible for me to achieve. In working towards that vision, as I am building the pieces required for it, those pieces should be independently useful for others. If I am not successful at any given point of my journey towards the final vision, what I have built up until that point should still be a net win for me in the work that I am doing at the time.

> Also, what about solutions like Purescript? I am guessing you want to go with Javascript due to its popularity and how easy it is to use? That certainly makes sense, just wondering hm!
I have considered compile-to-JS solutions, but I have issue with the community size, the ecosystem size, and the interop (they all promise 100% interop with JS on the box, but the reality is sometimes not so easy). Having said that, I'm likely to have the same challenges, there's more to interop than syntax, transpilation and module systems.
Mostly, though, the biggest concern I have is inheritability. I want a project that was created using my stack to be inheritable even to programmers that aren't necessarily familiar with FP. Although they may struggle to understand code they've inherited from me at first if they're not accustomed to FP, if that code is just a subset of JS, they can disable/remove the tooling that limits the code to a subset, and carry on with the project more easily using the paradigms that they are already familiar with.
I think this is one of the big issues with low-/no-code tools atm, they're walled gardens: there's literally no code, users are just configuring an instance of the vendor's platform. I want to build something that is genuinely inheritable, not just on paper, but in a very practical sense. (there are also many other problems with no-code that I hope to tackle).
It is for this reason that the entire stack needs to be open source, and its pieces should strive to be independently useful. I aim to build communities around each piece so that there will be some measure of support for those that do inherit from me.

# Martin Sosic 2020-06-16 08:38
Congrats :tada: :slightly_smiling_face:!

What is your reason for inheritability, do you think that will be the common case with the audience you are targeting?
I believe there is a reason why inheritability is not present in no-code / low-code tools -> because it is hard to achieve, and at the end for most of them it is not that important, due to the use case they are catering to. But of course, if they did have inheritability, they could target more / different use cases, since developers could use it for prototyping and similar.

With Wasp, we have been looking at two things: inheritability and extensibility.
Extensibility would actually be interop that you mentioned -> can we allow people to combine/use existing parts of JS ecosystem together with Wasp, intermingled with Wasp?
And inheritability -> can we allow people to "eject" the code made by Wasp, so they can continue with it? We are mentioning that on our landing page.
In our case, extensibility is a must, while we would really like to have inheritability because it removes the "lock-in" effect, but we are aware it might be hard to achieve it, and its importance will be smaller and smaller as Wasp becomes more and more mature, so we are still evaluating how much time does it make sense to invest into inheritability.
# Xandor Schiefer 2020-06-17 10:22
one of the problems with no code (and frameworks too, though not as strongly) is that if a project is successful, it will outgrow the initial platform on which its built. In the case of a framework, you can do a rewrite that still re-uses a lot of the existing code. In the case of a no-code platform, you have to rewrite from scratch.
The problem is that success for a lot of projects doesn't necessarily mean that there is money for a team of professional developers to rewrite. Success might mean that a no-code app is heavily relied upon internally, but the company couldn't afford to rewrite it.
No-code tools generally solve the problem of writing the code. But that's often all they solve. Syntax is not the hard part of coding (after a few months). The hard part is keeping things well organised and adaptable.
When a regular codebase gets out of hand, the code can be analysed and refactored with the help of tools. When a no-code app gets out of hand, :man-shrugging: . (I predict a healthy industry of useless no-code consultants that will help companies "maintain" no-code apps whilst making things worse, in about 5-10 years' time. Just look at sharepoint.)

I'm even hoping to have back-and-forth. If a developer writes code that adheres to the FP JS subset, I can generate a visual representation of it (the visual rep is just a projection of the AST, much like e.g. Prettier just projects the AST).
This would change no-code from a binary proposition (either yes or no), to a spectrum.
Citizen devs can make most of the "boring" parts of the app, and call in a pro when they get stuck. The pro can use the interface she's used to (text). It won't be a choice between sticking with a no-code ball of mud, or an expensive complete rewrite by a team of devs. Each party can play to their strengths.

If its not inheritable, it represents a lot of risk to companies. Vendor lock-in risk, rewrite risk, etc. which makes it less appealing. Knowing you can hit eject (or not even eject, but add a pro dev to a citizen dev team), will be an attractive proposition, and as you said, can also be used as a prototyping tool.
