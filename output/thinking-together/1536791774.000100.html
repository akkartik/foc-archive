# Stefan Lesser 2018-09-13 02:27
@Felix Kohlgrüber I like your question a lot. Don’t really have a good answer for it, but wanted to mention that my impression is that we all tend to try to build a big solution for better programming. That often leads to us being interested in what others do on the surface, but it rarely happens that our visions align enough so that we start collaborating (I tried to make that a little more explicit with my earlier effort to find shared principles for this group).

I wish it would be easier for us to extract smaller problems that we all have to solve on the way to a holistic solution, and maybe increase the chance of collaboration a little. I feel like we have built an awesome community of very capable individuals here that could pull off amazing things, if we can find ways to make us more than just the sum of the parts.

We should still all keep working on individual projects — diversity of ideas is very important in a space where we are mostly exploring. However, identifying the bits where more of us struggle with a concrete albeit smaller problem could tap into our collective power to solve it quicker or better or both.

Smaller artifacts like an explorable explanation of a concept more of us need to solve for their own projects could be a great way to collaborate for a defined amount of time and get some sense of achievement at the same time, which I’m sure will also have positive impact on our individual projects.

Of course, a first step in that direction would be to better understand what each of us are working on currently, and what some of the (smaller) research questions are we currently think about and maybe also make that information a little easier to find.

# Shalabh 2018-09-13 12:10
Great question, @Felix Kohlgrüber. There are definitely scenarios I think about, though it's not a small set but is ever-growing. My ongoing struggle is usually to reduce the set to the more general, broader problems. I'm approaching this from the POV that almost all concepts in computed are 'constructions/inventions' and if we start by rejecting them all, what is the set of concepts that we could introduce that would not lead to the kinds of problems we currently have. (Rejecting is easy but forgetting is hard). I think attacking the 'problem' at all levels is useful.
