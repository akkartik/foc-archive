# Chris Knott 2020-04-17 09:57
It's very impressive.

Are you able to explain a bit more about how the NLP works? For example, how does it know to map "Intercom messages" to "intercom.openChats"? Or how does it know to say "who are" customers, rather than "what is" customers etc?
# Steve Peak 2020-04-17 11:37
Thank you Chris! It’s about tokenizing the grammar then running it through our ML layer that we trained by us (it’s proprietary how) and by customers interacting with the product. Regarding Intercom messages: we know Intercom is a company and that company has vocabulary (messages, tickets, issues, chats etc) that can be mapped back to some “thing” (Chats, in this case).
Regarding, “who” well, our typesystem includes concepts of People, Locations, and other Things. It’s goes way beyond GPPL typesystems which have far less “generics” due to their generalization.
# nicolas decoster 2020-04-18 00:54
Woua! Very nice! One thing I find interesting with this approach, is that it forces you (as a designer of a programming system) to have high level programming constructs as close as possible to the "end-user" programmer interests. I mean, often in general purpose language, you have some kind of surrounding code (preparing variable, accessing data, etc., sometime boilerplate code) which is not that necessary. As programmers it can be part of our job to make this code less necessary and to produce higher abstractions, but with a system like the one in the video, you are forced to have these very high level constructs (and I understand it is at the core of the design of StoryScript).
