# Duane McLemore 2026-02-01 23:04
Hi all, renewing / updating my introduction. I'm Duane. I am currently an Assistant Professor of Architecture at Mississippi State University, where I teach architectural studios, computation, and representation.

About that Feeling of Computing. Even though we were really working class and in rural Indiana, I was lucky to grow up around personal computers. I was born at just the right time (1980) for that. I learned to "type" on my uncle's C64 before I could write with a pencil. When I was seven I wrote a LOGO program on my elementary school's Apple \\e to tile the monitor continuously with hexagons. My punchline is "and it's been downhill since."

Like most of you, the rest of my experience with computers is probably too much to relate without boring everyone. But for reasons I wound up not studying computers or software in college, instead pursuing a professional Bachelor's of Architecture. While using computers to design was a constant throughout my career (like anyone under 50 these days) - and I even wrote tons of little AutoLisp routines to make fun computational drawings - I was always a "computational design" thinker but otherwise automating that with computer languages wasn't something I dug back into extensively until I was about 30. It was then I started learning Grasshopper, a visual programming language inside the Rhino3d modeling software that was catching on then and is now extremely popular with students and practitioners alike.

Wanting to learn more about 3d printing and apply my rapidly growing skills in Grasshopper, my partner and I started Xover0 (X Over Zero). With her expertise and training in fashion and 2d design and mine in 3d design and geometry, we began designing jewelry in about 2014. We started selling it in 2016 from our small Koreatown LA back house. My use of and teaching of computational design in architectural education led me to realize I had a research question I wanted to answer, so I started applying for full time academic positions soon after.

In 2019 Katherine and I moved to Starkville, MS when I began teaching architecture at Mississippi State University. We bought a house (and have been renovating since). This gave us a place to let Xover0 flourish, and we have built out a fully featured casting studio (along with renovating dedicated office space, photo studio, and packaging area, etc). We now design and fabricate everything entirely in-house. If you know jewelry you know this is exceptionally rare.

The research question I referred to earlier is the realization that no one had made a tool to implement the 3d "Space Group" Symmetries for architectural computation. So I created the component library for grasshopper, named Horta, for this purpose. Since then I have also created Leafcutter, a proprietary library for the subdivision of concrete 3d prints into printable sized meshes.

Along the way I discovered APL and fell in love with the poetry of the syntax. (One day I'll refactor my component libraries into it). While it is no esolang, it was my gateway drug to them. Trying to find everything I could about APL and some computational subjects that I was teaching at the time, and with lots of time to listen to podcasts while doing home renovations, I found FoC. I'm guessing my first episode was Lickider or Engelbart because I was teaching students about those shortly before those were released, and I remember sharing them with the students later. Either way I was hooked - and it has only gotten better since. INTERCAL is still my favorite single podcast episode ever.

I submitted my dossier for Promotion and Tenure back in August, and no issues have come up to date. Once that is final I will be pursuing one of two PhD's - either writing control software for 3d printed concrete, or implementing some high powered geometry libraries in a novel way for analysis software.

Anyway - I'm glad to be a member of this community, and am absolutely floored by the level of intelligence and passion you all bring to things. If anyone sees something in the overly long screed above that you'd like to chat about or collaborate on, drop a line. And one of these days I'll be able to factor in supporting FoC on Patreon.

# Duane McLemore 2026-02-07 21:01
@Beni Cherniavsky-Paskin Regarding what specifically made it successful, that's down to a lot of diverse factors. Galo Canizares's paper is on Rhino3d more broadly, but it covers Grasshopper in fair detail.

https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pau.2024.100020

I would say that the big factors are that architects are "visual" thinkers and it caters to that. Also, your typical architect isn't versed in programming, so the fact that the iteration is abstracted away (C# does it, it just doesn't display any intermediate results) doesn't weird them out.

Another HUGE factor is the fact that the developers (McNeel) always published tooling and templates for developers to get started with writing for it. And it's in C# which is a straightforward language. Then, once people developed for it network effects kicked in. That is, people would see the results from some wild component library that had been fabricated, then jump in because they wanted to do things that looked like that.

The fact that it can do compiled libraries, "user objects" (bits of code in a built-in interpreter but that are pre-built) and totally custom code-in-node makes it usable at any level of formality.

And finally, the fact that it is much more straightforward than prior architectural computation tools - the text-based melscript and AutoLisp; and much more affordable than other graphic (or partially graphic) platforms like Generative Components for Microstation - means the barriers to entry were lower.

I'd love to continue this conversation, too!
