# Konrad Hinsen 2020-01-22 00:50
Nice presentation!
The point closes to my own interests is working on the edge between order and chaos. In computational science, that's my daily work, and it's a real problem that programming environments force me into one or the other. I have often said that Python's success in scientific computing is to its straightforward interfacing with C, which makes it a kind of bridge between the two worlds, though imperfectly.

I wonder where you would place some more recent arrivals on this scale. Clojure, for example, is a Lisp but with a strong accent on immutability. Unison seems fully on the "order" side in terms of your criteria, but feels much closer to a live programming environment.

Given that you want to concentrate on the "environment" aspect, did you consider working with the Unison team? They are concentrating on the "language" part and their language should be a nice fit for your environment ideas.

# Konrad Hinsen 2020-01-23 01:39
In a decent IDE (for Common Lisp, that would be Emacs+Slime, not sure what today's Clojure favorites are), a macro expansion is only one keyboard command away. Understanding it is a different story, if you haven't written the macro yourself. Still, most macros in practice are simple, and their expansion is understandable with reasonable effort.

Back to order vs chaos: I remember listening to a podcast episode not so long ago, where some Clojure guy (can't remember the name, sorry!) said that for some people, code is like literature, whereas for others it's like math. The preferences he quoted for each side are quite similar to what you call chaos and order, even though the point of view seems very different at first. My reaction to that analogy was that for me, code should be literature with embedded math.
