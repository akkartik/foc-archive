# Ivan Reese 2020-05-04 07:15
This was going to be a reply to Yair, but I decided to break it out so that more people would see it and have the chance to weigh in.

Here are my reasons for being against the creation of subject-specific channels. Please show me the error of my ways, or voice support, or suggest alternative ways we could support more subject-centric discussion that limits the bad kind of fervor.

• Most people here in this community are (or would benefit from being) interested in everything.
• Fewer channels are easier to follow, which is important if you are interested in everything.
• Every existing person will need to be told about each new channel. Lots of people check the slack infrequently and miss those messages, even after multiple repeats.
• Each new person will need to be told about all existing channels. We haven't yet found a good way to do this, other than including channels in the default set. Having a lot of default channels can be overwhelming. People already need to learn that things like #share-your-work, #CFQUMT7M3, and #two-minute-week have special rules, so I want to avoid making the newcomer experience even more intense.
• New people might not know that the point of subject-specific channels is that they're "safe spaces" for true believers :), so this will need to be communicated too.
• Whenever we do want to have a discussion about the merits of (say) FP vs logic programming, that stuff will still end up in #thinking-together, because the subject channels are safe spaces. So we've kept all our flame wars front-and-center and moved the more thoughtful discussions off to the sidelines.
• When posting something interesting that intersects multiple interests, you need to make a decision about which channel to post it in. Some people post to general. Some people post to a subject channel. Some do both. None of these are great.
