# em 2021-04-25 17:10
It's an interesting question, and one potential perspective is to see symbolic calculus as just augmented thought. The mathematical rigor and semantics of the system allows new discovery, but that isn't fundamentally different than more physical proxies. Many concepts in calculus aren't fundamentally difficult either when you break it down, and there's no real reason that children/adults of all ages would not be able to understand "difficult" symbolic logic when piecewise, the pieces are quite simple.

Given this is the future of coding slack, I think we realize the importance of well crafted, composable abstractions that enable us to augment our thinking (albeit in the computational context, an executable form of thought). If children were given blocks that could form other blocks, then there really wouldn't be a real barrier to something like calculus. I think one argument is that the threshold is if those pieces and tools they have allow that kind of expansion - something like pencil and paper certainly meets that threshold.

# Joshua Horowitz 2021-04-25 21:44
(tl;dr: http://worrydream.com/KillMath/)

I think Papert would believe that the most important things to get from calculus are the underlying concepts: "measure the rate of change of one quantity relative to another" and "accumulate one quantity as another changes".

These concepts give you a jaw-droppingly powerful language for understanding and modeling and predicting the world. I mean this qualitatively, but a rigorous understanding of this language also leads to differential equations, which underly essentially all dynamic systems.

When you say "solve problems like the area under a cycloid or parabola", my guess is you're talking about symbolically computing derivatives & integrals. This seems to me to be vastly less important. (Especially symbolic integration! I learned so many integration tricks in high school which I have literally never used, even throughout an undergraduate education in math and physics.)

You might not mean symbolic computation, though! You might mean knowing:

1. how a shape can be broken down into small pieces [strips below the parabola],
2. how this can be described as a relationship between quantities [strip height = f(x)],
3. how integration expresses the assembly of these small pieces [area = ∫ strip height × strip width = ∫ f(x) dx]
4. how hand-calculation or a computer program could approximate this integral.

I'm into all of these things, and I expect Papert would be as well.
# William Taysom 2021-04-25 22:30
So much to unpack. Won't do it justice.

(1) Calculus as a class combines two types of ideas: (a) theory of limits, derivatives, integrals, (b) practice of fairly sophisticated formula manipulation.

(2) A Mathland focuses on giving students a feel for (a) theory.

(3) One can master the (b) practice without a good feel for how the manipulations are related to (a) theory. A lot of time energy spent on the product rule, not so much in getting a really good feel for what it does.
# Jack Rusher 2021-04-26 07:23
There are many examples of children intuiting their way to these things in Kay's writing. I can vouch for this phenomenon as I ended up reinventing calculus at around eight years old to write little simulation programs using 1970s era 8-bit computers.

Expanding on what @William Taysom says above, not only can the common notation and formula manipulation tricks can be learnt with little or no real intuitive understanding, but likewise the intuition can be learnt quite well without that notation. I would even go on to say that in most regards an executable notation is superior to the 19th century standard.
